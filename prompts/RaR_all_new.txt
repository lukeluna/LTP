Hasty Generalization: Drawing a conclusion based on a small sample size.

Appeal to Ridicule: Mocking an argument to make it appear absurd without addressing its substance.
Appeal to Nature: Arguing that something is good because it is 'natural' or bad because it is 'unnatural'.
False Causality: Assuming that because two events occur together, one causes the other.
Ad Populum: Arguing that something is true because many people believe it.
Ad Hominem: Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.

False Dilemma: Presenting two options as the only possibilities when others exist.
Appeal to Fear: Using fear to persuade rather than logical argument.
Appeal to (False) Authority: Citing an authority on the subject who is not actually an expert.

Circular Reasoning: The conclusion is included in the premise, making the argument go in a circle.

Appeal to Anger: Using anger to persuade rather than logical argument.
Straw Man: Misrepresenting an argument to make it easier to attack.
Appeal to Tradition: Arguing that something is better because it is traditional.
Equivocation: Using ambiguous language to mislead or misrepresent the truth.
Fallacy of Division: Assuming what is true of the whole is true for the parts.
